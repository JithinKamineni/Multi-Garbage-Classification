Biodegradable waste consists of organic materials that can naturally decompose through the action of microorganisms such as bacteria and fungi. Common examples include food scraps, fruit peels, vegetable waste, garden waste, leaves, and other organic matter. Unlike plastic or metal waste, biodegradable materials break down naturally over time and can be converted into useful products such as compost.

Proper separation of biodegradable waste is important for effective waste management. Organic waste should be kept separate from recyclable materials such as plastic, glass, and metal. Mixing biodegradable waste with other materials can contaminate recyclable waste streams and reduce the efficiency of recycling processes.

Biodegradable waste is commonly processed through composting, a natural decomposition process that converts organic waste into nutrient-rich soil fertilizer. Composting can be performed at home using compost bins or through municipal organic waste collection programs. The compost produced from biodegradable waste can be used in agriculture, gardening, and landscaping to improve soil health.

When disposing of biodegradable waste, it should be placed in designated organic waste bins or compost containers. Avoid mixing biodegradable waste with non-compostable materials such as plastic bags or synthetic packaging.

Managing biodegradable waste effectively helps reduce the amount of waste sent to landfills. When organic waste decomposes in landfills without proper oxygen, it can produce methane gas, a potent greenhouse gas that contributes to climate change. Composting biodegradable waste instead allows organic matter to break down in a controlled environment that benefits soil ecosystems.

Through proper waste segregation and composting practices, biodegradable waste can be transformed from a disposal problem into a valuable environmental resource that supports sustainable agriculture and reduces environmental pollution.
